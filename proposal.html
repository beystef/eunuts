ECON 301 · Stage B Proposal

The Causal Impact of EU Aflatoxin Regulations on Global Nut Trade Pricing Dynamics

Yusuf Efdal Yılmaz

Current web version based on website/proposal.tex

Abstract

This project studies whether staggered European Union aflatoxin regulations causally affect bilateral export unit values in high-risk tree nuts and oilseeds. The empirical motivation is to distinguish between two channels: quality upgrading, where stricter screening raises observed unit values by filtering out lower-quality shipments, and trade destruction, where regulation acts as a barrier that reduces market access without clear quality gains. The project uses monthly bilateral HS6 export data, policy timing evidence, and a fixed-effects framework to examine how trade pricing responds to food-safety regulation.

1. Research Question and Motivation

The central research question is: what is the causal impact of staggered EU aflatoxin border regulations on the bilateral export unit values of susceptible tree nuts and oilseeds from major producing countries?

The motivation follows the increasing role of non-tariff measures in agricultural trade. As tariffs have declined, sanitary and phytosanitary measures and maximum residue limits have become central instruments shaping international market access. EU aflatoxin enforcement is a particularly relevant case because it is stringent, repeatedly enforced, and highly consequential for exporters in products such as hazelnuts, pistachios, almonds, peanuts, walnuts, and sesame.

Turkey is especially important in this context because it is the world’s leading hazelnut exporter and has faced repeated scrutiny in the EU over aflatoxin-related controls. This creates a substantive and policy-relevant setting for studying whether the regulation primarily improves quality composition or instead operates as a disguised trade barrier.

2. Literature Discussion

The proposal places the project in the broader literature on SPS measures, MRLs, and agricultural trade. A large share of this literature relies on gravity models to estimate aggregate trade costs. Those studies are useful for broad trade effects, but they are less suited for identifying product-level timing and price dynamics in bilateral pairs.

The proposal highlights three strands. The first studies general SPS and TBT barriers in agricultural trade. The second focuses specifically on aflatoxin regulation and developing-country exports. The third explores heterogeneity, adaptation, and quality upgrading under stricter standards. Across these strands, the key gap is that most papers rely on trade values or aggregate flows rather than unit values, making it difficult to separate quantity contraction from quality upgrading.

This project aims to contribute by using monthly bilateral variation and export unit values, with placebo products included to test whether any observed pricing response is specific to high-risk regulated products.

3. Data Plan

The data are designed as a high-frequency exporter-importer-product-month panel covering January 2000 to December 2023. Monthly frequency is especially useful because yearly aggregation could mask within-year timing around policy adoption and enforcement.

The treated products are hazelnuts, pistachios, almonds, and peanuts at the HS6 level. Walnuts and sesame seeds serve as placebo products because they share logistics and trade partners but face lower aflatoxin concern. Trade values and quantities come from UN Comtrade and are used to construct export unit values. Policy timing is intended to be assembled from TRAINS notifications and validated using RASFF border notifications. Tariff controls are planned through WITS or TRAINS.

4. Empirical Model and Specification Choices

The baseline model relates monthly export unit values to the currently active aflatoxin regime while controlling for bilateral tariffs and fixed effects. The proposal’s key coefficient is the pricing response to regulation. A positive sign is consistent with quality upgrading and selective compliance; a negative sign is more consistent with trade-destruction or barrier effects.

ln(UVijct) = β0 + Σd βd Policyijct(d) + β1 Tariffijcτ(t) + δic + λjt + θm + εijct

The log-linear form is chosen because unit values are positive and right-skewed, percentage interpretations are convenient, and tariffs can remain in levels without introducing arbitrary log transformations for zero values.

5. Endogeneity and Causal Interpretation

The proposal addresses causal interpretation through several diagnostics. First, an event-study design around the first policy date is intended to assess pre-trends and post-policy dynamics. Second, importer-time and exporter-commodity fixed effects absorb important confounding sources. Third, reverse causality is considered unlikely because EU regulation typically follows slow administrative and legislative processes rather than reacting to short-run price movements.

The proposal also notes measurement error in unit values and potential selection bias if some bilateral relationships exit under stricter regulation. A falsification exercise using low-risk placebo products is intended to test whether the estimated policy effects are specific to treated products rather than general macroeconomic shifts affecting European agricultural imports.
